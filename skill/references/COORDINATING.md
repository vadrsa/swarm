# Coordinating in place, and mining first

Background for the two stances at the top of SKILL.md. Nothing here is a new
instruction — the two stances are complete without this file. This is the *why*,
the two failures they exist to prevent, and what each looks like when done well.
Read it if you want to know whether a stance applies to your case; you do not
need it to obey them.

## The two failures these stances prevent

Both were watched, not hypothesized — the operator saw them in people picking
the tool up.

1. **The long-session dismissal.** `/swarm` fires in a session that has been
   developing for an hour. Two harms compound. The accumulated context leans the
   session to *dismiss* swarm for most things — an hour of momentum as a *doer*
   is an hour of "this is faster if I just do it." And the prior context, which
   is the single richest description of the work that exists anywhere, is never
   analyzed for how that work could be split. A biased judge, deciding whether to
   use the tool at all, while sitting on the best brief that will ever exist.

2. **The flat tree under the human.** Handing swarm tasks out of the box
   produces `human → N workers`. The human then hand-manages the whole flat
   tree: N panes, N reports, N judgments, N restarts. They wanted to manage *one
   thing* and instead they got a job.

Neither is a bug in any mechanism. Both are frame gaps — and the record here is
that **agents do not invent structural moves that are not in their frame, and
reliably execute the ones that are**. Standing up as the coordinator in place,
and mining your own context before you spawn, need no verb that does not exist.
They only needed saying.

## Why the seat is a tenure, not a posture

Why *stay* the coordinator, and not merely take the chair for a spawn burst:
after the burst, a session that read the seat as a posture drifts back to being a
doer, and the children's reports drift up to the human. Tenure is what stops the
drift.

**In place, with no extra hop.** The alternative — spawn a coordinator agent and
have the human talk to *that* — adds a layer whose only content is forwarding,
and a middle layer that only forwards is structure lying about work. It also
costs a hop of briefing fidelity for nothing. The session in front of the human
is already a Claude session holding the full context; making it the coordinator
is free, and adds no node: the human's session exists either way. The only
question is whether it delegates or grinds.

**Span is volume; topology is shape.** Doctrine 5 already protects the
operator's span — a claim about *volume* ("one ranked page", "default ~3"). The
inline stance names the *topology*: the human's default direct load is **one
node**, and that node is the session they are typing into. Two different claims;
you owe both. The failure they jointly prevent is the player-coach: a session
both grinding work and judging children does both worse.

**The seat is theirs to give.** The default protects the person who has not yet
learned what to ask for; the opt-out protects the person who has. A default the
human cannot see is a default they cannot refuse — which is why the stance names
its own refusal, and why the refusal is an instruction the session obeys rather
than a flag anyone sets.

**What this is not.** It is not an overseer or a load-balancer — not a node whose
job is other nodes' *behavior*. It is the node that **owns the work** and
delegates it, and it is the seat the human already occupies. And the motive is
never load: *what is scarce is the human's attention*, not the tool's capacity.
Three floods in this repo's record asked whether load summons a coordinator; it
does not, and agents reason their way out of one on cost grounds, correctly.
Argue this from attention or not at all.

## Why the map goes in the journal, and goes first

"Be less of a doer" is an attitude — unfalsifiable, unenforceable, and weak
against an hour of momentum. "Write the decomposition into your journal before
you spawn" is a structural move producing an inspectable file, which is the only
kind of instruction that reliably survives contact with a working session.

**The destination is what makes it real.** A session satisfies *"write it down"*
by typing into the chat — which is not an artifact, it is thinking aloud with a
period at the end. The journal is the repo's designated durable record: it is
what `swarm` itself points a compacted agent at, it costs zero new concepts, and
it turns "did you mine?" from a judgment about vibes into an `ls` and a read.

**Why the brief comes from the map.** A coordinator that ignores what its own
session learned briefs its children worse than a stranger would — and a
badly-briefed child is one you cannot judge well either, because a parent can
judge well precisely because it wrote the brief. Two notes on what the map holds:
the goal as this session *now* understands it (not as it was first stated — an
hour of work has probably moved it), and what was learned the hard way, stated so
a child does not have to re-learn it.

Note the motive: this is a claim about **briefing quality**, not about saving
context. Mining serves the goal. Fewer tokens is a side effect it is allowed to
enjoy and does not optimize for.

**It inverts the harm.** The dismissal reflex is strongest exactly when the
session knows the most — and the session that knows the most is precisely the one
best positioned to write a *good* decomposition. Mining spends that knowledge on
the tree instead of against it.

**Why compaction routes to the journal, and then to the human.** Compaction is the
one case the stance cannot fix by reading back — the working memory was summarized
away, so there is nothing to read back *to*. The journal is where the fallback
points because it is this tool's own designated repair for exactly that loss: it is
what `swarm` tells a restored agent to reach for. And where the journal comes up
short, the escalation is to ask rather than to reconstruct, because an honest
unknown beats a plausible wrong value — and a tree is the worst possible place to
guess. A wrong number is merely wrong; a hallucinated decomposition spawns agents
that then go and do wrong work. Note where the gate sits: at *"you still cannot
answer"*, not at *"you were compacted."* Compaction is a reason to read the journal,
never a licence to skip the mining.

## The edge this does not cover

All of this governs what happens **once `/swarm` has fired**. A session that
*should* have fired it and never did — handed its goal an hour ago, before the
goal had decomposed into anything, with nothing re-evaluating since — is out of
scope, and nothing in this repo re-evaluates it. Stated so the next reader does
not mistake it for solved.
